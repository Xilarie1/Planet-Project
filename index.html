Neptune

Neptune

Facts from Britannica

Neptune is the third biggest planet of the solar system and the eighth and outmost planet from the sun. It is too far from Earth to be seen with the naked eye, though it might be spotted with a small telescope.

Named after the Roman god of the sea, and identified with the Greek deity Poseidon, Neptune is the second planet to have been found by telescope. Its discovery combined Newtonian physics with a a numerological scheme which was not founded by science. Neptune's orbit is almost circular, unlike the nearby dwarf planet Pluto. This means that for 20 years of its 164 period revolution, Neptune is actually further away from the sun than Pluto.

Basic astronomy

With an orbital period 164.79 times Earth's, Neptune has circled the Sun once since it was discovered in 1846. We therefore expect astronomers to further refine calculations of its orbit well into this century. After Voyager 2 encountered the planet its distance has been revised and is now estimated at 4.5 Tm. (Terrametres) The shape of Neptune's orbit is the second closest to circular in our solar system. Only Venus has a more circular orbit. This circularity, in combination with the planet's rotational axis of 29.6° gives Neptune seasons of almost equal length, at approxomately 41 Earth years each.

The atmosphere

Like other giant planets, Neptune has a thick atmosphere composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with a small amount of methane. While hydrogen and helium are nearly invisible, methane absorbs red light and reflects blue light, and should give Neptune a blue-green colour similar to Uranus. However, Neptune's atmosphere is much more dynamic than Uranus', presumably due to an unidentified gas in the atmosphere.

As on Earth, the temperature in Neptune's atmosphere varies by altitude. The range is much greater, with a minimum temperature of 50 K, or -223 °C at a pressure of 0.1 bar. As the altitude increases the pressure decreases, so at 2 Mm above the 1 bar level, the temperature is 480 °C and the pressure is .1 µbar. Near the centre of the planet, the temperature is 7 kK, and the pressure may reach 5 Mbar. The whole planet has an effective temperature of -214 °C. The mean temperature at 1 bar is roughly 74 K, or -199 °C. The atmosphere is a little warmer at equator and the poles than mid-latitudes. Like other giant planets, the winds in Neptune's atmosphere circulate along constant lattitudes. Winds range from around 100 m⁄s at 70° S to 700 m⁄s at 20° S.

While Neptune is more than 50% further from the sun than Uranus and receives half as much sunlight, the two planets' effective temperatures are almost the same. Both planets absorb and reflect roughly the same proportion of the sunlight that reaches them. For reasons unknown, Neptune emits more than twice as much energy as it receives from the Sun, which must be generated in its interior. By contrast, little energy escapes Uranus.
